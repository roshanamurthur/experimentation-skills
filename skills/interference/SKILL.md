# Leakage and Interference between Variants

Standard experiment analysis assumes SUTVA — the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption: each unit's outcome depends only on its own variant assignment, `Y_i(z) = Y_i(z_i)`. When Treatment units interact with Control units through any shared medium (a friendship edge, an ad budget, a server's CPU), the assumption breaks, and the measured delta is biased — sometimes under, sometimes over. The single most important principle: identify the *medium* through which the variants interact, because the medium determines both the direction of the bias and the right fix.

## When to use

- The product has user-to-user interactions: messages, calls, feeds, invitations, likes, comments (social networks, Skype-style communication).
- Two-sided marketplace or auction: Treatment and Control compete for the same inventory, riders, drivers, or bids (Airbnb, Uber/Lyft, eBay).
- A shared budget or corpus: ad campaign budgets, ML relevance models trained on pooled engagement data.
- Shared infrastructure: same machines serve both variants (CPU, memory, caches, latency-shared services).
- Randomization unit is finer than user (pageview, session, query) and the Treatment has a learning/carryover effect on the user.
- An experiment's measured win "disappears" after launching to 100% — classic budget-constraint symptom.
- Choosing between geo, cluster, ego-network, or time-based (switchback) randomization designs.

## Core principles

1. **Interference = SUTVA violation, via direct or indirect connections.** Direct: two units share an explicit link — friends on a social network, people in the same physical space. Indirect: a latent shared resource connects them — one ad budget, one pool of rental inventory, one fleet of servers, one training corpus. Both create a medium through which Treatment leaks into Control. Diagnose the mechanism first; the solutions differ by medium.

2. **Direct-connection leakage usually biases the delta *downward* (you underestimate a good Treatment).** A better People-You-May-Know algorithm on LinkedIn makes Treatment users send more invitations; Control users receive them, come back, connect more — so total-invitations rises in *both* arms and the delta understates the true benefit. Same with messages (Control users reply) and Skype calls (better Treatment call quality → more calls → Control friends answer and call more). The Treatment lifts the whole ecosystem, and Control gets credit for part of it.

3. **Shared-resource competition usually biases the delta *upward* (you overestimate).** Treatment's gain is partly Control's loss, not new value: Airbnb — better Treatment conversion flow books inventory Control users then can't book, overestimating the effect; Uber/Lyft — a better surge algorithm takes drivers off the road, raising Control prices and suppressing Control rides; eBay — Treatment-encouraged higher bids make Control users lose auctions they'd have won; ad campaigns — Treatment clicks drain the shared budget, starving Control. A positive revenue delta during an ad experiment can become *neutral at launch* because the budget constraint binds either way. Budget-constrained experiments also read differently at the start of a month/quarter than at the end.

4. **Infrastructure and corpora leak too.** Same-machine serving: a Treatment bug that pins CPU/memory slows requests from *both* arms, so the latency damage is underestimated. Pooled model training: if Treatment and Control relevance models train on data from all users, "good" clicks generated by the Treatment model improve the Control model — the longer the experiment runs, the more Control benefits.

5. **Sub-user randomization leaks through the user.** Randomize by pageview with a Treatment that dramatically improves latency, and every user gets a mix of fast and slow pages; behavior on fast pages is influenced by slow ones and vice versa, underestimating the effect — the user is the latent connection. Use sub-user units only when there is no carryover/learning effect from the Treatment and success metrics are also sub-user (clicks-per-page, not sessions-per-user). Coarser corollary: features that act across a granularity level (personalization, query reformulation flowing from a Treatment first-page into a Control second query) invalidate randomizing at that level, and user-level metrics cannot be computed under page-level randomization.

6. **Cheap first check — the ecosystem-value rule of thumb.** Not every action spills over. On a plain Bernoulli-randomized experiment, instrument downstream-response metrics alongside first-order ones: messages sent *and responded to*; posts created *and* the likes/comments they receive; likes/comments *and* number of creators receiving them. Positive first-order impact with no downstream movement → measurable spillover is unlikely; proceed with the standard analysis. Where downstream impact exists, establish per-action ecosystem values (e.g., how many sessions does one message from A generate for A plus A's neighbors) from historical experiments via an Instrumental Variable approach, and apply as a correction. Limitation: it's an average — a specific Treatment's marginal messages may carry above-average ecosystem impact.

7. **Isolation designs remove the medium; each trades power for purity.**
   - **Split the shared resource** by traffic allocation: 20% of traffic gets 20% of the ad budget; training data split by originating variant. Works when the split can be exact (budget, data). Fails for machines — hardware heterogeneity confounds a machine split. Beware unequal splits biasing the resource itself: a model trained on 5% of data vs. one on 95% is handicapped by the split, not the Treatment — one more reason to run 50/50.
   - **Geo-based randomization** when interference is spatially local (hotels competing for the same tourists, taxis for the same riders). Randomize regions; regions are plausibly isolated. Cost: sample size capped at the number of geos → high variance, low power; lean on variance-reduction techniques.
   - **Time-based randomization (switchback)** — at each time t, flip all traffic to Treatment or all to Control. Valid only when same-user-over-time carryover isn't a concern. Unit can be seconds to weeks; day-granularity yields only 7 points/week, usually too few. Strong day-of-week / hour-of-day patterns are expected — exploit them with paired t-tests or covariate adjustment. (Related: interrupted time series.)
   - **Network-cluster randomization** — partition the graph into clusters of likely-interfering nodes; randomize clusters as mega-units. Two limits: (a) real graphs are too dense for clean cuts — 10,000 balanced clusters on the full LinkedIn graph still left >80% of edges crossing clusters; (b) few mega-units → small effective sample size, and a variance–bias tradeoff: more clusters lowers variance but raises bias via worse isolation.
   - **Ego-network randomization** — build clusters of one "ego" plus its immediate "alters", and assign them separately: e.g., all alters to Treatment, half of egos to Treatment. Comparing Treatment egos to Control egos measures first-order plus downstream impact with better isolation and lower variance than generic clustering.
   - **Combine dimensions for sample size**: if interference is short-lived and the effect transactional, re-flip each cluster daily (cluster × time). Improve clusters by predicting where interference will actually flow — users don't message every neighbor, so cluster on the subgraph where messages are likely, not the full dense graph.

8. **Edge-level analysis measures the network effect instead of designing it away.** Bernoulli-randomize users, then label each interaction edge T→T, T→C, C→C, or C→T. Contrast T→T vs. C→C edges for an unbiased delta; also test for Treatment affinity (do Treatment users prefer messaging other Treatment users?) and whether Treatment-created actions earn higher response rates. Works when leakage flows through well-defined pairwise interactions (messages, likes).

9. **Even without a precise fix, monitor for interference — and let ramp phases catch the worst of it.** Alert on symptoms: e.g., if all ad revenue movement during an experiment comes from budget-constrained advertisers, the result will not generalize post-launch. Early ramp rings — employees first, then a single small data center (0.5–2% of traffic) — surface gross resource interference like a Treatment consuming all CPU or a slow memory leak before it contaminates the measurement ramp.

## Workflow

1. **Map the media.** List every channel connecting units: social edges, messaging, shared budgets, shared inventory/auctions, shared machines/caches, pooled training data, same-user-across-units. No medium → SUTVA plausible (the independent-shopper case) → standard analysis.
2. **Predict bias direction** per medium: ecosystem spillover through direct connections → delta biased down; shared-resource competition → delta biased up; shared infra latency → harm underestimated; pooled training → Control improves over time, delta shrinks with duration.
3. **Run the cheap check first**: Bernoulli randomization + downstream-response metrics (principle 6). First-order impact without downstream movement → stop here.
4. **Pick the design matched to the medium**:
   - Divisible resource (budget, training data) → split by allocation, prefer 50/50.
   - Spatially local competition → geo randomization + variance reduction.
   - Marketplace/system-wide state, no per-user carryover concern → switchback; pick a time unit giving enough sample points; pair/adjust for hour-of-day and day-of-week.
   - Social-graph spillover → ego-network randomization first; general cluster randomization if ego structure doesn't fit; cluster on the predicted-interaction subgraph, not the raw graph.
   - Pairwise-interaction leakage → Bernoulli + edge-level analysis (no special design needed).
   - Sub-user randomization proposed → confirm no learning/carryover and sub-user metrics only; otherwise randomize by user.
5. **Stack dimensions** (cluster × time, geo × time) whenever isolation leaves you power-starved.
6. **Instrument monitoring**: downstream-response metrics, budget-constrained vs. unconstrained segment breakdowns, infra guardrails (CPU, memory, latency on shared machines). Alert, don't just dashboard.
7. **Use the ramp** as an interference detector: employees → single data center at 0.5–2% → all data centers (see `../ramping/SKILL.md`).
8. **Sanity-check generalization before launch**: if the effect concentrates in a constrained segment (budget-capped advertisers, supply-capped markets), expect the launched impact to shrink toward neutral; also expect month-start vs. month-end result differences for budget-mediated experiments.

## Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why it happens | Detect / avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Reading a marketplace/auction win at face value | Treatment's gain is Control's loss (Airbnb inventory, Uber drivers, eBay auctions) | Delta overestimated; use geo/switchback designs; check whether launched impact matches experiment impact |
| Underselling a social/communication feature | Control receives and reciprocates Treatment-generated messages/invites/calls | Delta underestimated; check downstream-response metrics; use ego-network or edge-level analysis |
| Ad revenue win that vanishes at launch | Shared campaign budget drained by Treatment during the test; constraint binds either way at 100% | Split budget by traffic allocation; segment results by budget-constrained vs. not; expect start-of-month vs. end-of-month differences |
| Pooled model training | Both variants' models train on all users' data, so Treatment's good clicks improve Control | Split training data by variant; note longer runs *shrink* the measured delta |
| Serving variants from the same machines during a resource bug | Treatment pins CPU/memory, slowing Control requests too | Latency harm underestimated; infra guardrails per variant; early single-data-center ramp |
| Splitting machines to isolate infra | Machine heterogeneity confounds the comparison | Don't split hardware naively; isolate by data center in ramp instead |
| Unequal resource splits (5%/95% training data) | Split size itself biases the resource | Prefer 50/50 allocations when splitting resources |
| Pageview/session randomization with a carryover Treatment | The user is a latent connection across units | Underestimated effect; randomize by user unless no learning effect and metrics are sub-user |
| Expecting clean graph clusters | Social graphs are dense: 10k balanced LinkedIn clusters still had >80% cross-cluster edges | Accept partial isolation; cluster the predicted-interaction subgraph; consider ego-network design |
| Cluster/geo designs with too few mega-units | Effective n = number of clusters/geos | High variance; more clusters ⇒ less bias-free isolation (variance–bias tradeoff); add time dimension; apply variance reduction |
| Day-granularity switchback | Only 7 sample points per week | Use finer time units when practical; pair on hour-of-day/day-of-week |
| Applying ecosystem rule-of-thumb everywhere | It's an average from historical experiments | A given Treatment's actions may carry above-average ecosystem value; validate for large or unusual effects |

## Rules of thumb

- Direct-connection spillover (messages, invites, calls) → measured delta biased **down**; shared-resource competition (inventory, budgets, auctions, drivers) → biased **up**; shared-infra harm and sub-user carryover → harm/effect **under**estimated.
- No downstream-response movement on first-order wins → spillover unlikely; skip exotic designs.
- Split shared resources in proportion to traffic, and keep splits 50/50 to avoid biasing the resource itself.
- Geo and cluster designs: effective sample size = number of geos/clusters, not users — budget power accordingly.
- LinkedIn-scale graph, 10,000 balanced clusters → still >80% of edges cross clusters; perfect isolation is a myth.
- Switchback time unit: fine enough for adequate sample points ("day" gives only 7/week); always adjust for hour-of-day and day-of-week.
- Edge labels for edge-level analysis: T→T, T→C, C→C, C→T; unbiased delta from T→T vs. C→C.
- Budget-mediated experiments read differently at start vs. end of month/quarter.
- Ramp rings that catch infra interference: employees, then one data center at 0.5–2% traffic.
- Sub-user randomization: only with no carryover and sub-user metrics; chosen for power, paid for in leakage risk.
